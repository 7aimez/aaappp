What Are Minerals?

Minerals are inorganic elements that originate from the earth and cannot be made by living organisms. Plants absorb minerals from the soil, and animals get their minerals from the plants or other animals they consume.

Unlike vitamins, minerals maintain their chemical structure. They are categorized into two groups: macrominerals (needed in larger amounts) and trace minerals (needed in smaller quantities).

Essential Minerals

Your body requires these vital minerals to function optimally, each playing unique roles in your health.

Calcium

Ca — MACROMINERAL

Essential for strong bones and teeth, muscle function, nerve transmission, and blood clotting. The most abundant mineral in the body.

Iron

Fe — TRACE MINERAL

Critical for oxygen transport in blood, energy production, and immune function. Helps form hemoglobin and myoglobin proteins.

Magnesium

Mg — MACROMINERAL

Involved in over 300 enzymatic reactions. Supports muscle and nerve function, blood sugar control, and protein synthesis.

Zinc

Zn — TRACE MINERAL

Crucial for immune function, wound healing, DNA synthesis, and protein production. Supports growth and development.

Potassium

K — MACROMINERAL

Regulates fluid balance, muscle contractions, and nerve signals. Essential for maintaining healthy blood pressure.

Selenium

Se — TRACE MINERAL

Powerful antioxidant that protects cells from damage. Supports thyroid function and plays a role in DNA production.

Natural Sources

  • Dark leafy greens like spinach and kale provide iron, calcium, and magnesium
  • Nuts and seeds are rich in zinc, magnesium, and selenium
  • Dairy products offer calcium and phosphorus
  • Seafood delivers iodine, zinc, and selenium
  • Legumes provide iron, magnesium, and potassium
  • Whole grains contain manganese, phosphorus, and selenium

Why We Need Them

  • Build and maintain strong bones and teeth structure
  • Convert food into usable energy efficiently
  • Support immune system function and wound healing
  • Enable proper muscle contraction and relaxation
  • Maintain healthy nerve transmission signals
  • Regulate hormones and enzyme production
