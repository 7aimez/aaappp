Tectonic Hazard Comparison
Three tectonic forces. One is the biggest

Volcanoes

These big mountains filled with magma are deadly, localised mosters. They can destroy cities, and can cause catastrophic damage, but they can be discovered in advance by computers.

Lower threat

Earthquakes

Sudden and devastating, they emit seismic waves, that cause bulidings to break and fall over. They happen without warning, and can be bad if they are a high magnitude.

High threat

Tsunamis

Combines earthquake destruction with obliterating waves. They distrupt the place with an earthquake, then sends out a massive wall of water to destroy the place.

Greatest threat
The Argument
Why tsunamis are
the deadliest hazard

Double damage

Tsunamis don't just come as a wave. They happen because of earthquakes that start under the sea, meaning that not only is there a wave, but an earthquake as well. Two in one.

Speed & size

The waves that come from the earthquake travel at up to 800 km/h across the ocean and can detroy entire cities in minutes, giving the people almost no time to react.

Almost impossible to defend

Low down buildings that are near the coast will be completely destroyed. Sea walls can help, but even the 12 m barriers in Japan were detroyed by a wave in 2011. Even the new 18 m walls may still not be enough to defend them.

Destroys cities

The tsunami in Japan in 2011 made about 300,000 people homeless, and they needed food, water, shelter, and medicine on a scale few other natural hazards demand.

Case Study — Japan 2011
Tōhoku earthquake &
tsunami response

The 2011 disaster demonstrated the catastrophic chain-reaction a tsunami triggers — from immediate destruction to nuclear crisis to decades of recovery.

Immediate Response

Rescue & access

Rescue services and the army moved into the affected area rapidly, clearing roads and creating access paths. However, the enormous amount of silt deposited by the tsunami made counting deaths extremely difficult.

Immediate Response

Medical emergency

Many hospitals were destroyed or damaged. Field hospitals were set up and doctors and nurses were flown from across the country. Patients were evacuated far from the emergency zone for treatment.

Humanitarian Aid

Shelter & relief

The army built temporary shelters for over 300,000 displaced people. The Japanese Red Cross received over $1 billion in donations and distributed 30,000+ emergency relief kits and 14,000 sleeping kits.

Infrastructure

Transport restoration

Sendai Airport was restored through a joint effort with the US Army by 29 March. About 347 km of the 675 km Tōhoku Expressway linking the region to Tokyo was damaged — fully repaired and reopened by 24 March.

Long-term

Reconstruction begins

The government established a Reconstruction Design Council with a budget exceeding ¥23 trillion to rebuild houses and infrastructure in the worst-affected areas.

Long-term

Upgraded defences

The original 12 m tsunami barriers were replaced with 18 m high walls. However, scientists have warned this may still be insufficient against a similar-magnitude earthquake.

Nuclear crisis — Fukushima

A 20 km evacuation zone was declared around the Fukushima plant. Water was continuously poured into damaged reactors to cool melting fuel rods. By November 2011 the reactors were cooled and stabilised, but the long-term challenge of removing radioactive contamination from air, water and land remains. Former UK nuclear safety expert John Price estimated it "might be 100 years before melting fuel rods can be safely removed."

“Tsunamis combine the violence of an earthquake with the unstoppable force of the ocean — making them the single greatest tectonic threat to human life.”

From dual-impact destruction to near-impossible coastal defence, from mass displacement to triggering secondary disasters like nuclear meltdowns, the evidence from Japan 2011 makes the case unequivocal.
